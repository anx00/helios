## Main finding

This LFPG slice is good enough to study raw PWS behavior, update cadence, and short-term temperature drift, but it is not yet good enough to estimate a reliable "real hit rate" against the next METAR.

Reasons:

- Only 25 audit rows exist across all 45 stations
- Only 2 next-METAR groups exist in the entire export
- Both next-METAR groups miss, so the observed next-METAR hit rate is 0%, but the sample is too small to treat as meaningful
- Current-METAR comparisons are also shallow: most stations only have 1 comparable point

## Delay hypothesis

The current LFPG local dataset does not confirm or reject the "station is right with delay" hypothesis yet.

- next-METAR lead groups: 2
- row-level next-METAR hit rate: 0.0
- final pre-METAR hit rate: 0.0
- late convergence groups: 0

This does not mean delay is absent. It means the export currently has too little supervised lead data to measure it.

## Practical read

- For immediate research, LFPG is useful for analyzing cadence, volatility, persistence, and candidate station families
- LFPG is not yet useful for claiming a trustworthy next-METAR accuracy percentage
- The Wunderground cluster looks better for dense delay studies
- The MADIS_APRSWXNET stations look more sparse and more jumpy, and should probably be evaluated separately from the Wunderground cluster

## Next steps

- Extend the local LFPG sample to at least 2-3 weeks before ranking stations by next-METAR hit rate
- Keep WUNDERGROUND and MADIS_APRSWXNET in separate leaderboards
- Add an analysis pass that groups rows by next METAR timestamp and measures time-to-convergence once more lead samples exist
